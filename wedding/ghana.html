Ghana Tradition

Ghana: A vibrant country on the west coast of Africa. The official language is English because it is a former British Colony. There are many local (tribal) languages most popular of which is Twi (pronounced like "twee", rhymes with "tree").

Population: Ghana has a population of around 34 million people.

What it is known for: Ghana is famous for being the first sub-Saharan African country to gain independence (in 1957). It's also known for its rich history, including the ancient Ashanti Kingdom and its gold coast era. You might have heard of its beautiful beaches, national parks teeming with wildlife, and its friendly and welcoming people. It's also a major producer of cocoa. It is a costal country and has some of the historical British castles and slave ports; a haven for tourists and a home or rich black/african culture.

Top Exports: Ghana's main exports include gold, cocoa beans and cocoa preparations, and crude petroleum. Timber and other agricultural products are also important.

Describe the people: Ghanaians are generally known for their warmth, hospitality, and strong sense of community. They are diverse, with many different ethnic groups, each with its own unique language, customs, and traditions. Family plays a very important role in Ghanaian society.

Now popular for: Ghana's "Year of Return" in 2019 marked 400 years since the first enslaved Africans arrived in the United States, significantly boosting tourism and inviting the diaspora to reconnect with their heritage. Additionally, December in Ghana, often called "Detty December," has become a major cultural and festive highlight, drawing visitors from around the world with vibrant celebrations, music festivals, and a strong sense of African identity.

Akan Marriage and Tradition

The Akan people, including the Ashanti and Fante (Michaelâ€™s tribe), have a rich and significant tradition surrounding marriage. From their perspective, marriage isn't just about two individuals coming together; it's a union between two families.

High Level:

Traditionally, when a man is interested in marrying a woman, he doesn't directly propose to her initially. Instead, his family plays a crucial role. They will first investigate the woman's family background and character. If they find her suitable, the man's family will formally approach the woman's family to express their interest. This initial step is often done through intermediaries (spokespeople). The woman's family will then also conduct their own inquiries about the man and his family.

Tradition:

The process involves several stages and ceremonies that emphasize the involvement and agreement of both families. It's a gradual process built on respect and negotiation between the two kinship groups. The focus is on building a strong foundation not just for the couple, but also for the relationship between their extended families.

Traditional Marriage (Joining of Two Families, Gathering, and Gifts)

The concept of traditional Ghanaian marriage strongly emphasizes the joining of two families.

Joining of Two Families:

In the Ghanaian tradition, it's viewed as creating a new bond and alliance between two lineages. The families become interconnected, sharing responsibilities and offering mutual support. Decisions about the marriage often involve the elders and other key family members from both sides.

Gathering:

Once both families have agreed to the marriage, a formal ceremony (or series of ceremonies) takes place. This often involves a gathering of extended family members, friends, and community members from both sides. The atmosphere is usually festive, with music, dancing, and feasting. These gatherings serve to publicly acknowledge and celebrate the union of the two families.

Gifts from the Man's Family to the Woman's:

A significant part of the traditional marriage process involves the presentation of gifts from the groom's family to the bride's family. These gifts are symbolic and vary depending on the specific ethnic group and their customs. They often include:

  • Bride Price (Dowry): This is a customary payment made by the groom's family to the bride's family. It's not seen as "buying" the woman but rather as a token of appreciation for raising her and a sign of the groom's commitment and ability to care for her. It can include money, drinks (like schnapps or local gin), clothing, jewelry, and other items.
  • Other Gifts: Beyond the bride price, the groom's family may also present other gifts to specific members of the bride's family, such as her parents, siblings, and other relatives. These gifts further solidify the bond between the two families and show respect for the bride's lineage.
  • Akontasikan: This is a specific and significant gift presented by the groom's family to the bride's brother (or the most senior male relative in her maternal lineage if she doesn't have a brother). The word "Akonta" means brother-in-law in Akan. This gift acknowledges the important role the bride's brother plays in her life and in the family. It can be money, cloth, or other valuable items. The Akontasikan symbolizes the groom's appreciation and respect for the bride's family and seeks to establish a good relationship with her male relatives, particularly her brother, who will often continue to have a protective and supportive role in her life even after marriage.
The Bag of Items for the Bride:

As part of the traditional marriage rites, the groom's family provides the bride with a collection of items to take with her to her marital home. These items are practical, symbolic, and intended to help her start her new life. The contents of this bag can vary slightly depending on the specific family traditions and the economic status, but often include:

  • Clothing (Ntoma): Several pieces of traditional cloth, which are essential for various occasions and daily wear.
  • Headscarf (Duku): Matching or complementary headscarves to go with the clothing.
  • Sandals or Slippers: For everyday use.
  • Toiletries: Soap, sponges, and other personal care items.
  • Cooking Utensils: Basic items needed for preparing meals in her new home.
  • Household Items: Sometimes, this can include bedding or other small necessities for the home.
  • Money: A sum of money to help her with initial expenses in her new household.
  • Jewelry: Occasionally, symbolic pieces of jewelry might be included.

Events

Kokooko (Knocking) and the Traditional Home Marriage:

The journey towards traditional marriage often begins with a custom called "Kokooko," which literally translates to "knocking."

Kokooko (The Initial Approach):

This is the formal initial step where the groom, accompanied by his family members (often including elders and a spokesperson), visits the bride's family home. The purpose of this visit is to formally announce the man's interest in marrying their daughter. They don't come empty-handed; they typically bring gifts such as drinks (like schnapps or palm wine) and sometimes a token amount of money.

The List of Items:

During the "Kokooko" visit, after the initial greetings and introductions, the groom's family formally states their intentions. The bride's family will then, if they are agreeable to considering the proposal, present a list of items (including the bride price and other customary gifts) that the groom's family will need to provide for the actual traditional marriage ceremony. This list is a result of tradition and discussion within the bride's family.

The Traditional Home Marriage:

Once the groom's family has gathered all the items on the list, an agreed-upon date is set for the traditional marriage ceremony. This event typically takes place at the bride's family home. It's a significant gathering where the groom's family formally presents the agreed-upon items to the bride's family in front of witnesses from both sides. The acceptance of these items signifies the official agreement and blessing of the marriage by both families. The ceremony involves prayers, exchange of vows (sometimes symbolic), and celebrations with food, music, and dancing. The bride is often formally handed over to the groom's family at this point.

So, to summarize the progression: it often starts with the "Kokooko" (knocking) where intentions are declared and a list of requirements is given. After the groom's family gathers these items, the traditional home marriage ceremony takes place, where the gifts (including the bride price and the Akontasikan for the brother) are presented, and the bride is given her bag of items for her new home. This entire process underscores the communal nature of marriage in Akan/Ashanti culture, highlighting the union of two families rather than just two individuals.
